# Abdominal Pain Clinical Guidelines

## Red Flags - Require Urgent Assessment
- Severe sudden onset pain
- Signs of peritonism (guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness)
- Abdominal distension with vomiting
- Haematemesis or melaena
- Fresh rectal bleeding with haemodynamic instability
- Fever with severe abdominal pain
- Pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA)
- Pregnancy with abdominal pain
- Jaundice with fever and RUQ pain (Charcot's triad)

## Differential Diagnosis by Location

### Right Upper Quadrant
- Biliary colic
- Acute cholecystitis
- Hepatitis
- Pneumonia (referred pain)
- Peptic ulcer disease

### Epigastric
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Gastritis
- GORD
- Pancreatitis
- Myocardial infarction (referred)

### Left Upper Quadrant
- Splenic pathology
- Gastric ulcer
- Pancreatitis

### Right Lower Quadrant
- Appendicitis
- Ovarian pathology
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Mesenteric adenitis

### Left Lower Quadrant
- Diverticulitis
- Ovarian pathology
- Constipation
- IBS

### Generalised/Central
- Gastroenteritis
- Intestinal obstruction
- Mesenteric ischaemia
- IBS
- Constipation

## Investigations

### First-Line
- Full blood count (FBC)
- CRP / ESR
- Urea and electrolytes
- Liver function tests (LFTs)
- Amylase or lipase
- Urinalysis
- Pregnancy test (all women of childbearing age)

### Second-Line
- Abdominal X-ray
- Erect chest X-ray (for perforation)
- Ultrasound abdomen
- CT abdomen/pelvis
- Upper GI endoscopy
- Colonoscopy

## Specific Conditions

### Gastritis/Peptic Ulcer Disease
**Investigations:**
- H. pylori testing (urea breath test, stool antigen, or serology)
- Upper GI endoscopy if red flags

**Treatment:**
- PPI: Omeprazole 20mg BD or Lansoprazole 30mg BD for 4-8 weeks
- H. pylori eradication if positive:
  - Triple therapy: PPI + Amoxicillin 1g BD + Clarithromycin 500mg BD for 7 days
  - Penicillin allergy: PPI + Metronidazole 400mg BD + Clarithromycin 500mg BD
- Lifestyle: avoid NSAIDs, reduce alcohol, stop smoking
- Review any causative medications

### Biliary Colic
**Features:** RUQ pain after fatty meals, lasting 1-5 hours
**Investigations:** Ultrasound abdomen, LFTs
**Treatment:**
- Analgesia: Paracetamol, Diclofenac 75mg
- Consider elective cholecystectomy

### Acute Cholecystitis
**Features:** RUQ pain, fever, Murphy's sign positive
**Investigations:** Ultrasound, FBC, CRP, LFTs
**Treatment:**
- Hospital admission
- IV antibiotics: Co-amoxiclav or Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole
- Cholecystectomy within 72 hours if possible

### Acute Appendicitis
**Features:** Central pain migrating to RIF, nausea, fever
**Investigations:** FBC, CRP, CT abdomen if uncertain
**Treatment:**
- Surgical appendicectomy
- IV antibiotics: Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole

### Diverticulitis
**Features:** LIF pain, fever, altered bowel habit
**Investigations:** FBC, CRP, CT abdomen
**Treatment - Uncomplicated:**
- Oral antibiotics: Co-amoxiclav 625mg TDS for 5 days
- Or Ciprofloxacin 500mg BD + Metronidazole 400mg TDS
- Clear fluids initially, then low residue diet

**Treatment - Complicated:**
- Hospital admission
- IV antibiotics
- Possible surgical intervention

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
**Diagnosis criteria:** Recurrent abdominal pain at least 1 day/week, associated with defecation, change in stool frequency or form
**Treatment:**
- Dietary advice: regular meals, adequate fluids, limit caffeine
- Low FODMAP diet trial
- Antispasmodics: Mebeverine 135mg TDS or Hyoscine butylbromide 10mg TDS
- For constipation: Linaclotide, or Laxatives
- For diarrhoea: Loperamide PRN
- Psychological therapies: CBT, hypnotherapy
- Antidepressants: Low-dose Amitriptyline 10-30mg nocte

### Gastroenteritis
**Features:** Diarrhoea, vomiting, abdominal cramps
**Treatment:**
- Oral rehydration (ORS)
- Antiemetics: Ondansetron 4mg or Metoclopramide 10mg
- Antibiotics NOT routinely needed
- Antibiotics if: Campylobacter (severe) - Azithromycin 500mg daily for 3 days

## Safety Netting
Advise patients to seek urgent care if:
- Pain becomes severe or unbearable
- Vomiting blood or passing blood in stool
- Unable to keep fluids down for 24 hours
- Abdominal swelling or distension
- Fever above 38.5°C
- Signs of dehydration (dizziness, reduced urine output)
- Pain spreading or worsening
- Collapse or feeling very unwell

## Follow-up
- Review within 48-72 hours if symptoms not improving
- Urgent 2-week referral if red flag symptoms for malignancy
- Routine gastroenterology referral for persistent symptoms
