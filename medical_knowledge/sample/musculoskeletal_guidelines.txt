# Musculoskeletal Pain Clinical Guidelines

## Red Flags - Require Urgent Assessment

### Back Pain Red Flags
- Cauda equina syndrome: saddle anaesthesia, bladder/bowel dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness
- Spinal cord compression
- Age <20 or >55 with new onset
- History of malignancy
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fever with back pain
- IV drug use
- Immunosuppression
- Thoracic pain
- Severe night pain
- Progressive neurological deficit

### Joint Pain Red Flags
- Hot, swollen joint with fever (septic arthritis)
- Trauma with inability to weight bear
- Significant swelling following injury (haemarthrosis)
- Locked joint

## Lower Back Pain

### Assessment
- Duration: acute (<6 weeks), subacute (6-12 weeks), chronic (>12 weeks)
- Neurological examination: straight leg raise, power, sensation, reflexes
- Red flag screening

### Investigations
- Usually NOT indicated for acute mechanical back pain
- MRI spine if: red flags, radiculopathy not improving after 6 weeks
- X-ray: if trauma, osteoporosis suspected, or chronic pain

### Management - Acute Non-Specific Back Pain
**First-line:**
- Reassurance and advice to stay active
- Paracetamol 1g QDS (limited evidence but safe)
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or Naproxen 500mg BD
- Add PPI if NSAID use prolonged: Omeprazole 20mg daily

**Second-line:**
- Codeine 30-60mg QDS (short-term only)
- Diazepam 2-5mg TDS for muscle spasm (max 5 days)

**Adjuncts:**
- Heat or ice packs
- Physiotherapy referral
- Avoid bed rest

### Sciatica Management
- Same as above PLUS:
- Consider neuropathic agent: Amitriptyline 10-75mg nocte or Gabapentin 300-1200mg TDS
- Epidural steroid injection if persistent
- Surgical referral if: cauda equina, progressive deficit, or persistent >6 weeks

## Neck Pain

### Assessment
- Range of motion
- Neurological examination of upper limbs
- Spurling's test for radiculopathy

### Management
**Acute mechanical neck pain:**
- Paracetamol 1g QDS
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS
- Soft collar only if severe (short-term)
- Heat application
- Gentle exercises and stretching
- Physiotherapy

**Cervical radiculopathy:**
- Add Amitriptyline 10-50mg nocte or Gabapentin
- Physiotherapy
- Consider MRI if not improving

## Shoulder Pain

### Differential Diagnosis
- Rotator cuff tendinopathy/tear
- Subacromial impingement
- Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis)
- Acromioclavicular joint pathology
- Glenohumeral osteoarthritis
- Referred pain (cervical spine, cardiac)

### Investigations
- X-ray shoulder if trauma or chronic symptoms
- Ultrasound shoulder
- MRI if rotator cuff tear suspected

### Management
**Rotator cuff tendinopathy:**
- Rest from aggravating activities
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or Naproxen 500mg BD
- Physiotherapy - strengthening exercises
- Corticosteroid injection: Triamcinolone 40mg + Lidocaine (max 3 injections)

**Frozen shoulder:**
- Analgesia: Paracetamol, NSAIDs
- Physiotherapy and home exercises
- Corticosteroid injection
- Hydrodilatation if not responding
- Duration typically 18-24 months

## Knee Pain

### Differential Diagnosis
- Osteoarthritis
- Meniscal tear
- Ligament injury (ACL, MCL)
- Patellofemoral pain syndrome
- Bursitis (prepatellar, infrapatellar)
- Gout/pseudogout
- Septic arthritis

### Investigations
- X-ray knee (weight-bearing if OA suspected)
- MRI if internal derangement suspected
- Joint aspiration if effusion (microscopy, culture, crystals)

### Osteoarthritis Management
**Non-pharmacological:**
- Weight loss if overweight
- Exercise and physiotherapy
- Walking aids if needed
- Appropriate footwear

**Pharmacological:**
- Topical NSAIDs: Ibuprofen gel, Diclofenac gel - apply TDS
- Paracetamol 1g QDS
- Oral NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or Naproxen 500mg BD (+ PPI)
- Capsaicin cream 0.025% QDS

**Interventional:**
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injection: Triamcinolone 40mg
- Hyaluronic acid injections
- Joint replacement if severe

## Hip Pain

### Differential Diagnosis
- Osteoarthritis
- Trochanteric bursitis
- Referred pain from spine
- Avascular necrosis
- Labral tear
- Fracture

### Management
**Trochanteric bursitis:**
- NSAIDs
- Physiotherapy
- Corticosteroid injection: Triamcinolone 40mg

**Hip osteoarthritis:**
- Similar to knee OA
- Walking stick (opposite hand)
- Hip replacement if severe

## Soft Tissue Injuries

### PRICE Protocol (First 48-72 hours)
- **P**rotection - avoid further injury
- **R**est - relative rest from aggravating activities
- **I**ce - 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours
- **C**ompression - elastic bandage
- **E**levation - above heart level

### Medication
- Paracetamol 1g QDS
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS
- Topical NSAIDs for local injuries

### Follow-up
- Physiotherapy referral for persistent symptoms
- Gradual return to activity

## Safety Netting
Advise patients to seek urgent care if:
- Sudden inability to move a limb
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Numbness around genitals or bottom (saddle area)
- Weakness in both legs
- Severe pain not controlled by medication
- Hot, red, swollen joint with fever
- Pain following significant trauma
- Unable to weight bear

## Referral Criteria
- Red flag symptoms
- Suspected septic arthritis (same day orthopaedics)
- Progressive neurological deficit
- Failed conservative management (typically 6-12 weeks)
- Consider joint replacement for severe OA affecting quality of life
