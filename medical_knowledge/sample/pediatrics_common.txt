# Pediatric Common Presentations & Triage

## Pediatric Chest Pain
### Overview
Chest pain in children is common but **rarely cardiac** in origin (<1% of cases).
Most common causes are Musculoskeletal (costochondritis) or Respiratory (cough/asthma).

### Differential Diagnosis
- **Musculoskeletal**: Costochondritis, chest wall strain (reproducible with palpation).
- **Respiratory**: Asthma, pneumonia, severe cough.
- **Gastrointestinal**: Reflux (GORD).
- **Psychogenic**: Anxiety, stress.

### Red Flags (Referral Required)
- Exertional chest pain (pain *during* exercise, not after).
- Syncope (fainting) with exercise.
- Known congenital heart disease.
- Significant family history of early cardiac death (<40 years).
-Abnormal ECG findings.

### Management
- If no red flags: Reassurance and simple analgesia (Paracetamol/Ibuprofen).
- Safety netting: Return if pain becomes severe, associated with palpitations, or syncope occurs.

## Fever in Children (NICE Traffic Light System)
### Green (Low Risk)
- Normal color skin.
- Responds to social cues, content/smiling.
- Stays awake or awakens quickly.
- Strong cry / normal breathing.
**Action**: Manage at home with antipyretics and hydration.

### Amber (Intermediate Risk)
- Pallor (pale skin).
- Not responding normally to social cues.
- Wakes only with prolonged stimulation.
- Decreased activity.
- Tachypnoea (RR >40 in 12-24mo).
**Action**: Face-to-face clinical assessment required.

### Red (High Risk)
- Pale/Mottled/Ashen/Blue skin.
- No response to social cues.
- Cannot be woken or stays awake only when stimulated.
- Weak, high-pitched or continuous cry.
- Grunting or chest indrawing.
- Non-blanching rash.
**Action**: Urgent referral to Paediatrics / Emergency Department.

## Bronchiolitis
### Overview
Viral Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI) common in infants <2 years. Usually caused by RSV.
### Symptoms
- Coryzal prodrome (runny nose) for 1-3 days.
- Followed by persistent cough and tachypnoea (fast breathing).
- Poor feeding (less than 50-75% of normal).
### Management
- Supportive care: Hydration and small frequent feeds.
- Monitor O2 saturations (admit if <92%).
- **Do NOT use**: Antibiotics, Salbutamol, or Steroids (ineffective).
