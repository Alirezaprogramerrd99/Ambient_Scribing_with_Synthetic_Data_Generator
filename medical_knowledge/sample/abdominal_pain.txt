# Abdominal Pain Assessment Guidelines

## Overview
Abdominal pain is one of the most common presenting complaints in primary care 
and emergency settings, requiring systematic evaluation to identify serious causes.

## Red Flags - Urgent Referral Required
- Severe pain with rigidity (peritonitis)
- Pain with haemodynamic instability
- Abdominal distension with vomiting (obstruction)
- Pain with PR bleeding and weight loss
- Pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA)
- Pain in pregnancy (ectopic)

## Location-Based Differential Diagnosis

### Right Upper Quadrant
- Biliary colic / Cholecystitis
- Hepatitis
- Pneumonia (referred)
- Peptic ulcer disease

### Epigastric
- Gastritis / GERD
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Pancreatitis
- Myocardial infarction (referred)

### Left Upper Quadrant
- Splenic pathology
- Gastritis
- Pneumonia (referred)

### Right Lower Quadrant
- Appendicitis
- Ovarian pathology
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Mesenteric adenitis

### Left Lower Quadrant
- Diverticulitis
- Ovarian pathology
- Constipation
- Inflammatory bowel disease

### Generalised
- Gastroenteritis
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Mesenteric ischaemia
- Bowel obstruction

## Initial Assessment
1. Vital signs including temperature
2. Abdominal examination - inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation
3. Digital rectal examination if indicated
4. Pregnancy test in women of childbearing age

## Investigations
- FBC, U&E, LFTs, Amylase/Lipase
- CRP
- Urinalysis
- Pregnancy test
- Abdominal X-ray if obstruction suspected
- CT abdomen if serious pathology suspected
- Ultrasound for biliary/renal/gynaecological causes

## Management Principles
- Analgesia: Paracetamol 1g QDS, consider opioids for severe pain
- Anti-emetics: Ondansetron 4mg or Metoclopramide 10mg
- IV fluids if dehydrated or vomiting
- NBM if surgical cause suspected
- Antibiotics if infection suspected

## Safety Netting
Advise patient to return if:
- Pain significantly worsens
- Develops fever or rigors
- Unable to keep fluids down
- Blood in vomit or stool
- Becomes confused or drowsy
