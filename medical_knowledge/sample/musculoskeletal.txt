# Musculoskeletal Assessment Guidelines

## Overview
Musculoskeletal complaints are among the most common reasons for GP consultations, 
including back pain, joint pain, and soft tissue injuries.

## Red Flags - Urgent Referral Required

### Back Pain Red Flags
- Cauda equina syndrome: bilateral leg weakness, saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention
- Spinal cord compression: progressive weakness, sensory level
- Spinal infection: fever, IV drug use, immunosuppression
- Malignancy: age >50 with new onset, weight loss, history of cancer
- Vertebral fracture: significant trauma, osteoporosis, steroid use

### Joint Red Flags
- Hot, swollen joint with fever (septic arthritis)
- Acute monoarthritis (gout, pseudogout, septic)
- Joint instability following trauma

## Common Presentations

### Low Back Pain
- Mechanical (95%): worse with movement, better with rest
- Radicular: dermatomal leg pain, worse sitting
- Inflammatory: morning stiffness >30 mins, improves with activity

### Shoulder Pain
- Rotator cuff: painful arc, weakness on testing
- Frozen shoulder: global restriction, especially external rotation
- Acromioclavicular: localised tenderness, pain on cross-body adduction

### Knee Pain
- Osteoarthritis: mechanical pain, crepitus, bony swelling
- Meniscal: locking, giving way, joint line tenderness
- Ligamentous: instability, effusion post-trauma

### Hip Pain
- Osteoarthritis: groin pain, reduced internal rotation
- Trochanteric bursitis: lateral hip pain, tender over trochanter

## Initial Assessment
1. History: onset, mechanism, aggravating/relieving factors
2. Inspection: swelling, deformity, muscle wasting
3. Palpation: tenderness, warmth, effusion
4. Range of motion: active and passive
5. Special tests as indicated
6. Neurovascular examination

## Investigations
- X-ray: trauma, suspected OA, persistent symptoms
- MRI: soft tissue pathology, suspected disc disease
- Bloods: FBC, CRP, ESR, urate if inflammatory cause suspected
- Joint aspiration: suspected septic arthritis or crystal arthropathy

## Management

### Acute Soft Tissue Injury (PRICE)
- Protection
- Rest (relative)
- Ice (20 mins, 4-6 times daily)
- Compression
- Elevation

### Analgesia Ladder
1. Paracetamol 1g QDS (first-line)
2. NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or Naproxen 500mg BD
3. Topical NSAIDs for localised pain
4. Consider weak opioids short-term for severe pain

### Physiotherapy
- Refer for persistent symptoms (>6 weeks)
- Exercise programmes for chronic conditions
- Manual therapy as appropriate

### Injections
- Corticosteroid injections for inflammatory conditions
- Limited evidence for long-term benefit
- Max 3 injections per joint per year

## Safety Netting
Advise patient to return if:
- Pain significantly worsens
- Develops weakness or numbness
- Unable to weight-bear (lower limb)
- Signs of infection (fever, redness, swelling)
- Symptoms not improving after 6 weeks
