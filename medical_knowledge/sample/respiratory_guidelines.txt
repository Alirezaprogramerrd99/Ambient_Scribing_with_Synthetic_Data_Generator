# Respiratory Conditions Clinical Guidelines

## Cough Assessment

### Red Flags
- Haemoptysis (coughing up blood)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Night sweats
- Persistent cough >3 weeks with risk factors
- Stridor or severe breathing difficulty
- Chest pain with breathing
- Fever >38.5°C with productive cough

### Differential Diagnosis
- Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI)
- Acute bronchitis
- Pneumonia
- Asthma
- COPD exacerbation
- Post-nasal drip
- GORD-related cough
- ACE inhibitor-induced cough
- Lung cancer
- Tuberculosis

## Investigations

### First-Line
- Oxygen saturation (SpO2)
- Peak expiratory flow rate (PEFR)
- Chest X-ray (if pneumonia suspected or cough >3 weeks)
- Full blood count
- CRP (C-reactive protein)

### Second-Line
- Spirometry (FEV1, FVC, FEV1/FVC ratio)
- Sputum culture and sensitivity
- CT chest
- Bronchoscopy
- Tuberculosis testing (Mantoux, IGRA)

## Acute Bronchitis

### Management
- Usually viral - antibiotics NOT routinely indicated
- Supportive care: rest, fluids, paracetamol
- Honey and lemon for symptomatic relief
- Consider delayed antibiotic prescription

### When to Prescribe Antibiotics
- Systemically unwell
- Pre-existing comorbidities (COPD, heart failure, immunocompromised)
- CRP >100mg/L
- Antibiotic choice: Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5 days
- Penicillin allergy: Doxycycline 200mg day 1, then 100mg daily for 5 days

## Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

### CURB-65 Score
- Confusion
- Urea >7mmol/L
- Respiratory rate ≥30/min
- Blood pressure <90 systolic or ≤60 diastolic
- Age ≥65 years

### Treatment by Severity
**Low severity (CURB-65 0-1):**
- Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5 days
- Alternative: Doxycycline 200mg then 100mg daily

**Moderate severity (CURB-65 2):**
- Amoxicillin 500mg TDS + Clarithromycin 500mg BD
- Or Co-amoxiclav 625mg TDS

**High severity (CURB-65 3-5):**
- Hospital admission required
- IV Co-amoxiclav + IV Clarithromycin

## Asthma

### Diagnosis
- Variable symptoms: wheeze, breathlessness, cough, chest tightness
- Diurnal variation - worse at night/early morning
- Triggers: exercise, allergens, cold air, infections
- Spirometry: FEV1/FVC <0.7, reversibility ≥12%
- Peak flow variability >20%

### Stepwise Management
**Step 1:** SABA as needed (Salbutamol 100mcg 2 puffs PRN)
**Step 2:** Add low-dose ICS (Beclometasone 200mcg BD or Budesonide 200mcg BD)
**Step 3:** Add LABA (Formoterol or Salmeterol) or increase ICS
**Step 4:** Increase ICS to medium dose, add LTRA (Montelukast 10mg)
**Step 5:** High-dose ICS, specialist referral, consider biologics

### Acute Asthma Attack
- Salbutamol 4-6 puffs via spacer (or nebulised 5mg)
- Prednisolone 40-50mg oral for 5 days
- Oxygen to maintain SpO2 94-98%
- Ipratropium bromide nebulised if severe

## COPD

### Diagnosis
- FEV1/FVC <0.7 post-bronchodilator
- Smoking history usually >10 pack-years
- Chronic symptoms: dyspnoea, cough, sputum

### Management
**Bronchodilators:**
- SABA: Salbutamol PRN
- LAMA: Tiotropium 18mcg once daily
- LABA: Salmeterol 50mcg BD or Formoterol

**Inhaled Corticosteroids:**
- Add if frequent exacerbations (≥2/year)
- Triple therapy: ICS + LABA + LAMA

**Other:**
- Smoking cessation (most important intervention)
- Pulmonary rehabilitation
- Annual influenza and pneumococcal vaccination
- Long-term oxygen therapy if PaO2 <7.3kPa

### COPD Exacerbation
- Increase bronchodilators
- Prednisolone 30mg daily for 5 days
- Antibiotics if purulent sputum: Amoxicillin or Doxycycline
- Hospital admission if severe

## Safety Netting
Advise patients to return or seek urgent care if:
- Breathing becomes more difficult
- Lips or fingers turn blue
- Confusion or drowsiness
- Unable to complete sentences
- Coughing up blood
- High fever not responding to paracetamol
- Symptoms worsening despite treatment
- Chest pain develops
