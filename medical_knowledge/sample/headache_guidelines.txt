# Headache Clinical Guidelines

## Red Flags - Require Urgent Assessment
- Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap") - worst headache of life
- Headache with fever and neck stiffness (meningitis)
- Headache with papilloedema
- New neurological deficit
- Headache with altered consciousness
- New headache in patient >50 years
- Headache worse on coughing/straining/bending
- Progressive headache over weeks
- Headache with jaw claudication or scalp tenderness (GCA)
- Headache following head trauma
- Headache in immunocompromised patient
- Headache with early morning vomiting

## Differential Diagnosis

### Primary Headaches
- Tension-type headache
- Migraine (with or without aura)
- Cluster headache
- Medication overuse headache

### Secondary Headaches
- Subarachnoid haemorrhage
- Meningitis/encephalitis
- Intracranial tumour
- Giant cell arteritis (temporal arteritis)
- Idiopathic intracranial hypertension
- Cervicogenic headache
- Sinusitis
- Hypertensive emergency

## Investigations

### When to Investigate
- Red flag features present
- New headache pattern
- Treatment-resistant headache
- Neurological signs

### Tests
- Blood pressure measurement
- ESR/CRP (if GCA suspected - ESR often >50mm/hr)
- Full blood count
- CT head (urgent if SAH suspected)
- Lumbar puncture (after CT, for SAH or meningitis)
- MRI brain (tumour, venous thrombosis)
- MRA/CTA (aneurysm, dissection)

## Tension-Type Headache

### Features
- Bilateral, pressing/tightening quality
- Mild to moderate intensity
- Not aggravated by routine activity
- No nausea or vomiting
- May have photophobia OR phonophobia (not both)

### Treatment
**Acute:**
- Paracetamol 1g QDS (max 4g/day)
- Ibuprofen 400mg TDS
- Aspirin 900mg (avoid in under 16s)

**Preventive (if frequent >15 days/month):**
- Amitriptyline 10-75mg nocte (start 10mg, increase gradually)
- Lifestyle: stress management, regular sleep, exercise

## Migraine

### Features
- Unilateral, pulsating headache
- Moderate to severe intensity
- Aggravated by physical activity
- Nausea and/or vomiting
- Photophobia AND phonophobia
- Duration 4-72 hours untreated
- Aura in 25%: visual disturbance, sensory symptoms

### Acute Treatment
**Mild-Moderate:**
- Aspirin 900mg + Metoclopramide 10mg
- Or Paracetamol 1g + Metoclopramide 10mg
- Or Ibuprofen 400-600mg

**Moderate-Severe:**
- Triptans: Sumatriptan 50-100mg oral, or 6mg subcutaneous
- Alternatives: Zolmitriptan 2.5-5mg, Rizatriptan 10mg, Almotriptan 12.5mg
- Can repeat triptan after 2 hours if needed
- Antiemetic: Metoclopramide 10mg or Prochlorperazine 10mg

**Contraindications to Triptans:**
- Ischaemic heart disease
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Previous stroke or TIA
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Pregnancy

### Preventive Treatment (if ≥4 migraines/month)
**First-line:**
- Propranolol 40-160mg daily (avoid in asthma)
- Topiramate 25-100mg daily (contraception required - teratogenic)
- Amitriptyline 10-75mg nocte

**Second-line:**
- Candesartan 8-16mg daily
- Sodium valproate 400-1500mg daily (avoid in women of childbearing age)
- Botulinum toxin (specialist)
- CGRP monoclonal antibodies: Erenumab, Fremanezumab (specialist)

## Cluster Headache

### Features
- Severe unilateral orbital/supraorbital pain
- Duration 15-180 minutes
- Autonomic features: lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, ptosis, miosis
- Restlessness/agitation during attack
- Occurs in clusters over weeks-months

### Acute Treatment
- Oxygen 100% at 12-15L/min via non-rebreather mask for 15 minutes
- Sumatriptan 6mg subcutaneous injection

### Preventive Treatment
- Verapamil 240-960mg daily (requires ECG monitoring)
- Short course Prednisolone 60-100mg tapering over 2-3 weeks

## Medication Overuse Headache

### Features
- Headache ≥15 days/month
- Regular overuse of acute headache medications for >3 months
- Simple analgesics: ≥15 days/month
- Triptans/opioids/combination analgesics: ≥10 days/month

### Treatment
- Withdraw overused medication (can do abruptly or gradually)
- Warn: withdrawal headache expected for 2-10 days
- Support: antiemetics, hydration
- Consider preventive medication
- Limit acute treatments to <10 days/month

## Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

### Features
- Age >50 years
- New headache, often temporal
- Scalp tenderness
- Jaw claudication
- Visual disturbance (risk of blindness)
- Polymyalgia symptoms

### Investigation
- ESR (usually >50mm/hr)
- CRP elevated
- Temporal artery biopsy (gold standard)
- Temporal artery ultrasound

### Treatment - START IMMEDIATELY IF SUSPECTED
**Without visual symptoms:**
- Prednisolone 40-60mg daily

**With visual symptoms:**
- IV Methylprednisolone 500mg-1g daily for 3 days
- Then oral Prednisolone 60mg daily
- Urgent ophthalmology referral

## Safety Netting
Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention if:
- Sudden severe headache (worst ever)
- Headache with fever and stiff neck
- Headache with confusion or drowsiness
- Headache with weakness, numbness, or speech problems
- Visual disturbances with headache (especially if over 50)
- Headache following head injury
- Headache with persistent vomiting
- Headache not responding to usual treatment

## Follow-up
- Review migraine prophylaxis at 8-12 weeks
- Headache diary recommended
- Review medication use to prevent overuse headache
