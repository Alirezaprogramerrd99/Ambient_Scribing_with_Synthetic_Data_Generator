# Cardiology Assessment and Management

## Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
### Presentation
- **Typical Pain**: Central, crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm, jaw, or neck.
- **Associated Symptoms**: Sweating (diaphoresis), nausea, shortness of breath, palpitations.
- **Silent MI**: Common in elderly or diabetic patients (may present as confusion or just breathlessness).

### Management (Pre-hospital/ED)
1. **MONA Protocol**:
   - **M**orphine (if severe pain).
   - **O**xygen (only if SpO2 < 94%).
   - **N**itrates (GTN spray 2 puffs, repeat if necessary).
   - **A**spirin (300mg chewable).
2. **Investigation**: 12-lead ECG immediately.

## Heart Failure (Acute Decompensated)
### Symptoms
- Severe breathlessness (orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea).
- Pink frothy sputum.
- Fluid overload (ankle oedema, raised JVP).
### Physical Signs
- Bilateral basal crackles on lung auscultation.
- Third heart sound (S3 gallop).
### Management
- IV Furosemide (Diuretic).
- Oxygen (titrated).
- Sit patient upright.

## Atrial Fibrillation (AF)
### ECG Findings
- "Irregularly irregular" rhythm.
- Absent P-waves.
- Variable ventricular rate (often tachycardic).
### Management Rules
- **Rate Control**: Beta-blockers (Bisoprolol) or Calcium Channel Blockers (Diltiazem).
- **Rhythm Control**: Considered if young, symptomatic, or new onset (<48 hours).
- **Stroke Prevention**: Calculate CHADS2-VASC score to determine need for anticoagulation (DOAC or Warfarin).

## Basic ECG Interpretation Guidelines
- **Rate**: Calculate 300 / number of large squares between R-R.
- **Rhythm**: Is it regular? Are P-waves present?
- **ST Elevation**: Sign of STEMI (Heart Attack). Look for >1mm elevation in limb leads or >2mm in chest leads.
- **ST Depression/T-wave inversion**: Signs of Ischaemia or NSTEMI.
- **Prolonged PR Interval**: First-degree heart block (>0.2s).
