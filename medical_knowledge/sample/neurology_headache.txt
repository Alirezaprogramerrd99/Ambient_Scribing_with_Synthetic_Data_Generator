# Neurology and Headache Assessment

## Headache Assessment
### Red Flags (SNOOP4)
- **S**ystemic symptoms (fever, weight loss).
- **N**eurologic symptoms (confusion, focal weakness, seizure).
- **O**nset sudden ("Thunderclap" headache - max intensity <1 min).
- **O**lder age (>50 years with new onset).
- **P**attern change (progressive, worse with valsava/cough/bending).
- **P**recipitated by valsava.
- **P**ostural aggravation.
- **P**apilloedema.

## Migraine (Without Aura)
### Diagnostic Criteria (At least 5 attacks)
- **Duration**: 4-72 hours.
- **Characteristics** (at least 2):
  1. Unilateral location.
  2. Pulsating quality.
  3. Moderate or severe pain intensity.
  4. Aggravation by routine physical activity.
- **Associated Symptoms** (at least 1):
  1. Nausea and/or vomiting.
  2. Photophobia and Phonophobia.

### Management
- **Acute**: Triptans (Sumatriptan 50-100mg) + NSAID (Ibuprofen/Naproxen) taken early.
- **Prophylaxis** (if impact is high): Propranolol, Amitriptyline, or Topiramate.
- **Lifestyle**: Headache diary to identify triggers (sleep, stress, caffeine).

## Tension-Type Headache
### Characteristics
- Bilateral location.
- Pressing or tightening (non-pulsating) quality.
- Mild or moderate intensity.
- Not aggravated by routine physical activity.
- **No** nausea or vomiting.

### Management
- Reassurance.
- Simple analgesia (Paracetamol/Aspirin).
- Stress management / Physiotherapy for neck tension.

## Medication Overuse Headache
- Occurs in patients with pre-existing headache disorder.
- Regular overuse of acute headache medication for >3 months (e.g., triptans/opioids >10 days/month).
- **Management**: Abrupt withdrawal of overused medication (warn patient of worsening symptoms for 1-2 weeks).
